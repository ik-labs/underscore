# Director's Notes — The River

## Tone

This film is not about grief as collapse. It is about grief as weather — something that arrives, changes the landscape, and slowly passes. The score must never comfort the audience too early. We earn the light at the end.

Do not resolve tension musically before the picture resolves it. Let the discomfort breathe.

## Sonic World

The river is the film's heartbeat. Every scene near water should feel like the sound design and the score are the same organism. I want the audience to be unsure where the location sound ends and the music begins.

Key reference textures (do not reference these artists by name — translate them into abstract descriptors):
- Slow, bowed string drones that feel like they are decaying from the inside
- Single piano notes with long reverb tails — not melodic, more like stones dropped into water
- Low sub-bass rumble that you feel in your chest before you hear it
- Occasional distant brass — muted, as if heard through fog or walls
- Silence used as a musical element — do not fill every moment

## Scene-Specific Notes

### Pre-dawn river bank (opening)
Silence first. Then, very gradually, a single low string note emerges — it should feel like it was always there and we are only now noticing it. No percussion. No melody. Just texture and the sound of the river.

### Kitchen scene (morning after)
The fluorescent light hum should be part of the score. The music, if present, should be almost inaudible — felt more than heard. Perhaps nothing at all. Trust the silence and the clock.

### Bridge scene (rain)
The rain is the percussion track. Build the score around it. As Mara looks over the railing, consider a brief swell — not triumphant, not dramatic, just present. Like a held breath.

### Grief group (community hall)
No score. Only ambient room sound and rain. The absence of music here makes the music elsewhere more powerful.

### River at night (turning point)
This is the emotional centre of the film. The moon on the water. The tin box. She does not throw it in — that restraint is the whole film. The music here should be the most sustained and developed cue. Slow build. Let it unfold over three or four minutes. End quietly — not with resolution, but with acceptance.

### Final field (spring)
The first major key cue in the film. But keep it humble. A few notes. Morning light sound. The listener should feel it as a quiet exhalation, not a triumph.

## Instrumentation Preferences

- Small string ensemble (quartet or quintet) — no full orchestra
- Prepared piano or heavily reverbed upright piano
- Field recordings woven into the mix (water, rain, wind)
- Avoid: drums, rhythm-forward writing, melodically bright moments until the final scene

## Pacing

Never rush. This is a film about someone learning to stay present with loss. The music must model that. Long durations. Slow development. Allow notes to die naturally.

## Avoid

- Sentimentality — do not tell the audience how to feel
- Swelling orchestral resolutions before the picture earns them
- Anything that feels borrowed from other grief films
- Melodic hooks — this score lives in texture and atmosphere
